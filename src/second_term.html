Formulas Used in This Topic

1. Expected Value of a Discrete Random Variable

\[ E[X] = \sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i p_i \] Where:
• \(X\): random variable representing uncertain outcomes.
• \(x_i\): the i-th possible outcome of \(X\).
• \(p_i\): probability associated with outcome \(x_i\).
• \(n\): number of possible outcomes.
Context: Represents the long-run average outcome under repeated identical conditions.

2. Variance of a Discrete Random Variable

\[ \mathrm{Var}(X) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} (x_i - E[X])^2 p_i \] Where:
• \(x_i\): possible outcome.
• \(E[X]\): expected value of the distribution.
• \(p_i\): probability of outcome \(x_i\).
• \(n\): number of outcomes.
Context: Measures dispersion and quantifies risk or volatility.

3. Expected Monetary Value (EMV)

\[ EMV(A_j) = \sum_{i=1}^{m} p_i \,\text{Payoff}(A_j,S_i) \] Where:
• \(A_j\): decision alternative (e.g., a strategy).
• \(S_i\): state of nature (future scenario).
• \(\text{Payoff}(A_j,S_i)\): monetary result of \(A_j\) under state \(S_i\).
• \(p_i\): probability of state \(S_i\).
• \(m\): number of states of nature.
Context: Represents the average monetary performance of a strategy under uncertainty.

4. Maximin Criterion

\[ \text{Maximin}(A_j) = \max_j \left( \min_i \,\text{Payoff}(A_j,S_i) \right) \] Where:
• \(A_j\): decision alternatives.
• \(S_i\): states of nature.
• \(\min_i \text{Payoff}(A_j,S_i)\): worst-case payoff of \(A_j\).
• Outer max selects the highest worst-case result.
Context: Represents a conservative decision maker seeking safety.

5. Maximax Criterion

\[ \text{Maximax}(A_j) = \max_j \left( \max_i \,\text{Payoff}(A_j,S_i) \right) \] Where:
• Inner max: best payoff each alternative can achieve.
• Outer max: selects the alternative with the largest best-case payoff.
Context: Represents an optimistic decision maker seeking maximum possible gain.

Example 1

Use a payoff table to compare options across the same states of nature. EMV multiplies each payoff by its probability and adds the results. Maximax focuses on the highest possible payoff, while Maximin focuses on the strongest worst-case payoff.

Example with diverging recommendations: A firm has three technology strategies under three demand scenarios (payoffs in $000s).
State of Nature Probability Innovate (A) Improve (B) License (C)
High demand 0.30 150 110 70
Moderate demand 0.40 80 90 70
Low demand 0.30 -20 40 60
EMV calculations: \[ EMV_A = 0.30(150) + 0.40(80) + 0.30(-20) = 71k \] \[ EMV_B = 0.30(110) + 0.40(90) + 0.30(40) = 81k \] \[ EMV_C = 0.30(70) + 0.40(70) + 0.30(60) = 67k \] EMV recommends Improve (B). Maximax (step-by-step reasoning):
The Maximax rule selects the option with the highest possible payoff.

Step 1: Identify the best payoff for each strategy. \[ \begin{aligned} \max_i \text{Payoff}(A, S_i) &= 150k\\ \max_i \text{Payoff}(B, S_i) &= 110k\\ \max_i \text{Payoff}(C, S_i) &= 70k \end{aligned} \] Step 2: Choose the largest of these best values. \[ \max\{150, 110, 70\} = 150k \] Conclusion: Maximax recommends Innovate (A). Maximin (step-by-step reasoning):
The Maximin rule selects the option with the strongest worst-case payoff.

Step 1: Identify the worst payoff for each strategy. \[ \begin{aligned} \min_i \text{Payoff}(A, S_i) &= -20k\\ \min_i \text{Payoff}(B, S_i) &= 40k\\ \min_i \text{Payoff}(C, S_i) &= 60k \end{aligned} \] Step 2: Choose the highest of these worst values. \[ \max\{-20, 40, 60\} = 60k \] Conclusion: Maximin recommends License (C). Because the three rules weigh risk differently, they point to different choices even though they use the same payoff table.
